Testicular Cancer

Overview

Testicular cancer is a type of cancer that begins in the testicles, part of the male reproductive system responsible for producing sperm and testosterone. While relatively rare, it is the most common cancer in young men between 15 and 35 years old. The good news is that it is highly treatable, particularly when detected early. At Healthsphere, we emphasize awareness and early intervention to improve survival and recovery rates.

Causes

The exact cause of testicular cancer is not fully understood, but several risk factors can increase the likelihood of developing the disease:

  • Undescended testicle (cryptorchidism): Men whose testicles did not descend properly during infancy are at higher risk.
  • Family history: Having a father or brother with testicular cancer raises the risk.
  • Age: Most common in young and middle-aged men, especially those aged 15–35.
  • Race: More common in white men compared to other ethnic groups.
  • Personal history: Men who have had cancer in one testicle are more likely to develop it in the other.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of testicular cancer include:

  • A painless lump or swelling in either testicle
  • Feeling of heaviness in the scrotum
  • Pain or discomfort in the testicle or scrotum
  • Changes in testicle size or firmness
  • Lower back, abdominal, or groin pain (in advanced stages)
  • Fatigue or unexplained weight loss (in advanced cases)

If you notice any of these symptoms, it is crucial to consult a healthcare professional for proper diagnosis and treatment.

Treatment

With proper care, testicular cancer is one of the most treatable cancers. Treatment options include:

  • Surgery (Orchiectomy): Removal of the affected testicle, often the first step in treatment.
  • Radiation therapy: Effective for certain types like seminomas, using high-energy rays to kill cancer cells.
  • Chemotherapy: Uses cancer-killing drugs, especially if cancer has spread.
  • Stem cell transplant: May be used in advanced or recurrent cases.

Treatment choice depends on the type, stage, and spread of cancer, as well as the patient’s overall health. At Healthsphere, specialists create personalized treatment plans for the best outcomes.

Prevention

There’s no guaranteed way to prevent testicular cancer, but some steps can lower risks and improve early detection:

  • Regular self-exams: Checking for lumps or unusual changes can help detect cancer early.
  • Awareness of risk factors: Knowing family history and personal risk can guide proactive screening.
  • Early medical consultation: Immediate attention for any changes improves treatment success.

While prevention is not always possible, early detection and timely treatment are the keys to recovery and long-term survival.
